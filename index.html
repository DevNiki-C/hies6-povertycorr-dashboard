🔍 Key Takeaways

Education is the single most impactful factor with the most perfect monotonic relationship with poverty. Literate households have up to 72% lower deep poverty rates than illiterate ones: The largest gap observed across all variables.

Land ownership also shows a strong monotonic relationship with poverty (ρ = −1.00, National & Rural) every step up in land holding corresponds to a reduction in poverty rate. Only Urban data disrupts the perfect reduction.

Urban areas consistently weaken every correlation whether positive or negative, suggesting cities provide structural buffers that partially decouple household characteristics from poverty outcomes.

Spearman rank correlation was used for the three continuous variables (Household Size, Age of Head, Land Ownership, Education) given their ordinal, non-normally distributed and unequally spaced category structures. Point-biserial correlation was used for all binary variables.

🔍 Interesting Insights

Rural households bear the sharpest burden of size. A rural household of 11+ members has a 32.9% UPL poverty rate: nearly 4× that of a 1–2 person household (8.7%). Spearman ρ = 0.94 in rural areas confirms a near-perfect rank-order relationship.

Urban poverty plateaus and even dips for very large households. The UPL rate for 11+ urban households (15.7%) is actually lower than 9–10 member households (28.1%), which is why Urban Spearman ρ (0.66) is weaker, the rank order breaks at the top end, likely because very large urban households pool resources.

LPL correlations are stronger than UPL for National and Rural (ρ = 0.83 vs 0.77 National), suggesting household size is an even better predictor of deep poverty than moderate poverty in those strata.

Age of Household Head Negative ρ
Older household heads tend to have lower poverty rates across most strata
🔍 Interesting Insights

Age is protective but the relationship is not strictly monotonic. The 30–39 age group actually has higher poverty rates than the ≤29 group in several strata, which is why Spearman ρ (−0.80) is strong but not perfect.

Rural deep poverty (LPL Rural, ρ = −0.87) shows the strongest age effect. Older rural heads have accumulated land, established farming networks, and reduced debt burdens over decades compounding the protective effect of age in a way urban settings cannot replicate.

Urban LPL (ρ = −0.10) is essentially zero. In cities, structural factors like occupation, education, and neighbourhood determine deep poverty far more than the age of the household head.

Land Ownership Negative ρ
More land consistently reduces poverty: The second most monotonic relationship in the dataset
🔍 Interesting Insights

Perfect Spearman ρ = −1.00 for National and Rural. Every single increase in land holding category corresponds to a reduction in poverty rate without a single exception. The most definitive finding in the entire analysis.

Landlessness is the single greatest rural poverty risk. Rural landless households face a 35.6% UPL and 16.6% LPL rate: the highest in the dataset. Even owning under 0.05 acres drops the UPL rate by 6.8 percentage points.

Urban breaks the perfect pattern (ρ = −0.82 UPL, −0.74 LPL) because households with 7.5+ acres show a slight uptick in poverty.

Education Status Literacy Reduces Poverty in all cases.
Literacy is associated with dramatically lower poverty rates: the most impactful variable
🔍 Interesting Insights

Education has the largest absolute poverty gap of all variables. Illiterate households face a 26.9% UPL rate, nearly double the 14.2% for literate households.

The literacy dividend is biggest in cities. Urban literate households have a 71.8% lower LPL rate than urban illiterates (8.5% → 2.4%).

The most perfectly negative monotonic relationship with poverty.

Illiteracy poverty rates are remarkably uniform across locations (~26.6–27.0% UPL) whether rural or urban, illiterate households face similar poverty odds. Literacy is a base condition whose absence cannot be compensated by location alone.

The gender poverty gap is real but limited to the upper poverty band. Male-headed households are 5.7pp more likely to be near-poor (UPL) in rural areas, yet LPL rates are identical for both genders nationally and rurally. The gender gap disappears at severe deprivation.
